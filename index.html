Who actually has solar? The income story the AEMC’s pricing review missed

The communities most exposed to the AEMC’s June 2026 network pricing reform are not the wealthy solar adopters the model implies — they’re regional and suburban Australia. New postcode-level analysis of solar installation data since 2010 shows the lowest-income decile has consistently had more solar than the highest-income decile, and the highest One Nation vote-share electorates are also the highest solar electorates.

The lowest-income decile (median ~$48k/year) has had higher overall solar penetration than the highest-income decile (~$133k/year) every year since 2010.

The AEMC’s rationale for raising shared network access charges is that consumers who can’t access solar — renters, apartment dwellers, and low-income households — are increasingly bearing network costs that solar owners avoid. The AEMC does not claim solar owners are uniformly wealthy. Its argument is about who is excluded from solar. But the reform’s instrument — shifting costs from per-kWh charges to a fixed access charge — does not distinguish between the non-solar groups it aims to protect. This postcode-level data tests whether the underlying income geography supports the reform’s logic.

The lowest-income decile has had higher overall solar penetration than the highest-income decile every year since 2010 — but the relationship is not simply “poorer = more solar.”
By 2025, the lowest-income decile had 47% solar penetration vs 40% for the highest-income decile. Decile 7 (upper-middle income) now leads at 47.3%. The gap between D1 and D10 peaked at 9.5 percentage points in 2019 and has been narrowing — but is far from closed.

Controlling for dwelling type: the income gradient reverses

A genuine question about the main finding here: is the inverse income-solar gradient simply an artefact of dwelling type? Wealthy inner-city postcodes have far more apartments than lower-income suburban and regional postcodes, and apartments cannot practically install rooftop solar. If that’s driving the pattern, then the story isn’t really about income at all.

The data confirms dwelling type as the primary explanation for the overall gradient — and this actually clarifies, rather than weakens, the argument against the AEMC’s premise.

All postcodes vs house-only postcodes

Among detached houses, the income gradient actually reverses: higher-income house-owning postcodes have more solar, not less.
Among postcodes dominated by detached houses, solar penetration rises from around 44% in the lowest-income decile to 50% in decile 9 and 48% in decile 10. The overall inverse gradient — lower income, more solar — largely reflects the concentration of apartments (which cannot install rooftop solar) in high-income inner-city postcodes. Remove the apartments, and the income-solar relationship flips. The AEMC’s premise that solar owners are wealthy is not wrong among house-owners; it is wrong when applied to the full population.

Tenure, not income, is the real structural barrier: renters at all income levels lack solar.
House-dominated postcodes with high renting (>30% renters) have 40% average solar penetration. Comparable postcodes with low renting (<20%) have 48% — a 7 percentage point gap. In D2 postcodes (median ~$57k/yr), high-renter areas have 27% solar vs 46% in low-renter areas: a 19 percentage point gap at a $145/year income difference. The AEMC groups renters, apartment-dwellers, and low-income owner-occupiers together as “non-solar households who can’t access CER.” The data says these are three separate problems requiring three separate policy responses.

The AEMC’s April 2026 distributional analysis explicitly acknowledges that reform will affect households differently. It models six “cameos” — representative households — under a 10-year transition to 80% shared network access charges. The results reveal a fault line that has nothing to do with whether you have solar: it’s about how much electricity you use.

The AEMC’s “fairness” argument is really an argument about consumption, not income.
High-consumption households (large families, electrified homes) save hundreds of dollars a year. Low-consumption households — including many elderly renters and single-person households — pay more, and the AEMC concedes they need targeted protection. But those “protections” are not part of the current rule-change proposal.

The cross-subsidy the AEMC diagnosed, and what the data actually shows

The AEMC’s core equity argument is that solar owners avoid network charges, shifting costs onto consumers unable to access consumer energy resources (CER) — a category the AEMC explicitly names as renters, apartment dwellers, and low-income households. The AEMC is not claiming solar owners are a wealthy elite; it is arguing that the people structurally excluded from solar are disproportionately disadvantaged. Our postcode data tests whether the instrument chosen to fix this — raising shared network access charges — actually tracks the population it’s meant to protect.

The AEMC is right that solar owners avoid some shared network costs. But the reform assumes the solar-owning group skews wealthy and the non-solar group skews poor. That income geography doesn’t hold at postcode scale. The lowest-income postcodes have had higher solar penetration than the highest-income postcodes for over a decade. The AEMC’s own April 2026 analysis modelled 25,000 households across 10 distribution networks, but did not cross-tabulate solar ownership against postcode income. This analysis does.

What this means for the policy: shifting costs from per-kWh charges to a shared access charge doesn’t distinguish between (a) wealthy inner-city non-solar apartment dwellers and (b) lower-income solar-owning suburban households. If the cross-subsidy runs substantially in both directions, raising access charges doesn’t fix the equity problem — it imposes new costs on lower-income households who already invested in solar to manage their bills.

The highest-solar electorates are in regional and suburban Australia, not the wealthy inner cities. The pattern holds at every scale.

The assumption that shifting costs away from per-kWh charges protects disadvantaged non-solar households is directly contradicted by the electoral geography of solar adoption.

One Nation vote share is the strongest single predictor of solar penetration across all 150 electorates (r = 0.43, p < 0.001).
The top One Nation electorates — Wright (13.8%), Capricornia (13.7%), Kennedy (12.1%), Flynn (11.8%) — average over 50% solar penetration. The lowest One Nation electorates (Wentworth 1.0%, Goldstein 1.2%, Curtin 1.2%) are the lowest-solar, highest-income seats. By contrast, ALP vote share has no significant correlation with solar (r = −0.08, not significant). The reform the AEMC frames as protecting “low-income non-solar households” would fall hardest on communities already expressing the highest political discontent over energy costs. What this means for the AEMC’s review

The AEMC’s June 2026 final report proposes a 10-year transition to tariff structures with up to 80% of network costs recovered through shared network access charges. The AEMC is right that the status quo has a distributional problem: as solar uptake grows, network costs are spread across a smaller volumetric base, and the households least able to reduce consumption — renters, apartment dwellers, low-income owner-occupiers — pay an increasing share. Inaction is not cost-free. But the instrument chosen to fix it matters.

This analysis challenges the income geography that underlies the reform’s logic. At the aggregate postcode level, the lowest-income decile has had more solar than the highest-income decile every year since 2010. The key reason is structural: high-income postcodes contain far more apartments, which cannot install rooftop solar. Among detached houses, higher-income households actually have more solar. The non-solar population is not a homogeneous low-income group — it includes wealthy inner-city apartment dwellers, lower-income renters, and house-owners who haven’t yet installed. The reform’s instrument — raising the unavoidable access charge — does not distinguish between these groups: it erodes returns for solar owners across all incomes while raising fixed costs for renters who cannot respond.

The AEMC’s own distributional modelling acknowledges that the reform helps high-consumption non-CER households while potentially harming low-consumption non-CER households and that consumer protections are a “critical design feature” and “necessary precursor” to any rule change. Those protections have not yet been specified. Before any rule change proceeds, the distributional modelling should be disaggregated by both CER ownership and household income — not just consumption. The two are not the same thing.

The policy ask

The AEMC should publish disaggregated distributional analysis crossing postcode income with solar ownership — not just consumption quintiles — before any rule change on shared network access charges proceeds. The data exists. Using it would show whether the equity case for reform holds at the household level, or whether the reform redistributes costs from wealthy non-solar apartment-dwellers to lower-income solar-owning suburban households, and allow consumer protections to be designed around the actual population at risk, not a proxy.
