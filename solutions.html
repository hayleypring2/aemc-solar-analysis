Three solutions the AEMC didn't fully consider

The AEMC identifies a real funding problem and three disadvantaged groups. But it proposes one instrument — higher network access charges — for all three. These solutions address each group directly, and produce better distributional outcomes without eroding solar returns.

Why the AEMC's instrument doesn't match its problem The AEMC correctly identifies three non-CER groups facing adverse outcomes: renters (excluded by tenure), apartment-dwellers (excluded by building type), and low-income owner-occupiers (allegedly excluded by cost). These are structurally different problems requiring different responses. A higher shared network access charge treats them identically — and our data shows the third group has already largely adopted solar.
①

Fix the renter exclusion directly

Community solar and minimum energy standards address the structural barrier — not the price signal.

②

Charge for the grid capacity you've contracted, not a flat per-connection fee. Costs track benefit received.

① Fix the renter exclusion directly

The AEMC's entire equity argument rests on the premise that renters are excluded from solar. That's true — but the solution is a property rights intervention, not a pricing reform. Renters pay the electricity bill. Landlords own the roof. Neither has an incentive to install solar under the current framework. A higher network access charge doesn't change that calculation at all — renters pay more and still can't get solar.

Nationally: 11% of renters have rooftop solar, vs 44% of owner-occupiers — a 33 percentage point gap.

ECA Consumer Energy Report Card (December 2025, n=4,535 nationally representative). The survey captures actual solar ownership, not installations — this is the lived experience of the tenure gap at a national scale. Renters in freestanding houses are better positioned than apartment dwellers, but even house renters face a structural barrier that pricing reform cannot address: they don't own the roof.

Community solar allows households to purchase a share of a nearby solar installation and receive the benefits on their bill — as if they had rooftop solar themselves. This applies to both renter-occupied houses (whose landlords may not install) and apartment-dwellers (where rooftop isn't possible). At realistic uptake rates:

What voluntary programs actually deliver: Solar Victoria's rental solar program reached 1.4% of renter houses over its entire lifetime.

Solar Victoria has offered a landlord solar rebate since 2019 (with the interest-free loan component added in 2020, reaching the current $1,400 rebate + $1,400 loan structure). By December 2025, the program had delivered solar to 4,900 rental properties — out of an estimated 348,134 renter-occupied separate houses in Victoria. That's 1.5% of all Solar Victoria solar installations going to rentals, and 1.4% of renter houses reached in total. Over the same period, 323,090 owner-occupier households received solar through the scheme. Owner-occupier solar penetration in Victoria now exceeds 35%.

The split incentive — landlords bear the install cost, tenants get the bill savings — is not fixed by making the rebate bigger. Only mandatory standards or structural credit mechanisms (like virtual net metering) change the underlying incentive. Voluntary rebates are a starting point, not a solution.

Two structural mechanisms to scale this

Minimum energy standards for rental properties. VIC is already doing this — phased standards from 1 March 2027 covering insulation, heating and hot water. NSW is in active consultation (submissions closed May 2026). The next step is adding solar-readiness or solar installation to these standards for separate houses, as QLD and VIC rebate programs have begun incentivising. Cost falls on landlords; tenants benefit from lower bills immediately. The ACT has operated mandatory energy rating disclosure for residential properties for over 20 years — the institutional precedent is well established.

Virtual net metering / community solar framework. Allow any renter or apartment dweller to subscribe to a share of a nearby solar installation and receive credits on their electricity bill — as Enova Community Energy already does in NSW on a small scale. The Australian Government's Community Solar Banks program ($100M) is funding this at larger scale. Requires a regulatory change to how solar credits are allocated under the National Electricity Rules, which the AEMC could initiate. This directly addresses both renter-houses and apartments in one mechanism.

Fixing the renter exclusion shrinks the AEMC's core problem substantially.

If 35% of Australia's 1.42 million renter-occupied houses gained solar access through community solar or tenancy standards, ~497,000 additional households would be in the solar economy. The pool of "consumers unable to access CER" — the AEMC's stated target for reform — would be dramatically smaller. The network funding problem doesn't disappear, but the equity argument for higher access charges becomes much weaker.

② Connection capacity pricing

The AEMC's access charge reform correctly identifies that network costs are mostly fixed — the grid has to be built and maintained regardless of how much electricity flows through it. But it proposes recovering those costs through a flat per-connection fee: every connected household pays the same daily charge, regardless of how much capacity they've actually contracted for or how large their peak draw is.

Connection capacity pricing is a more equitable alternative: charge based on the maximum capacity available at a household's connection point — the size of their fuse or meter in kilowatts. This is how many European electricity systems recover network costs. It means:

Flat access charge (AEMC's approach)

A single-person apartment with a small meter pays the same daily charge as a 5-bedroom house with EV charging, a 3-phase connection, and a home battery. The link between cost and benefit is severed.

Capacity-based charge (alternative)

The same apartment — with a small, single-phase 32A connection — pays a fraction of what the large house pays. The household that installed EV charging and expanded to 3-phase pays proportionally more. Costs track contracted benefit.

Who pays what: dwelling composition by income decile

Composition of the dwelling stock by income decile: owner-occupied houses with solar, without solar, renter-occupied houses, and apartments. Under a flat access charge, all pay equally. Under capacity pricing, apartments and smaller dwellings pay less. Source: ABS 2021 Census, APVI 2026.

Apartments concentrate in high-income deciles — making flat access charges regressive.

Apartments rise from 2.4% of dwellings in D1 to 14.1% in D10. Under a flat access charge, these small high-income apartments pay the same as large lower-income suburban houses. A capacity-based charge would mean apartments — which typically have smaller connections and lower maximum demand — pay less. The distributional effect runs in the right direction: lower charges for lower-capacity, typically smaller or lower-income households.

How capacity pricing compares to the AEMC's approach

The AEMC's own final report acknowledges that the access charge "may need to vary depending on the methodology deployed" and that charges "could vary according to aspects of a customer's historical consumption or a customer's characteristics." Connection capacity is precisely such a characteristic — and it's already metered. The AER and networks could implement capacity-based differentiation within the existing regulatory framework without a flat charge as the starting point.

Ausgrid already operates residential demand tariffs (EA111 and EA116) that charge based on maximum half-hourly demand during peak windows (3pm–9pm every day during peak seasons: June–August and November–March). New residential customers in Ausgrid's network are assigned to EA116 by default. The mechanism is not theoretical — it's live, operating under existing rules, and sits alongside the current network access charge. The question is not whether demand-based cost recovery can work in Australian networks. It's why the AEMC's reform doesn't build on it.

③ Peak demand charges

The AEMC is right that per-kWh charges don't track what actually drives network costs. But the alternative isn't necessarily a flat access charge — it's a charge that more directly measures what drives network investment: coincident peak demand.

The network is sized to handle the worst-case demand event — a hot February evening when every air conditioner in Queensland is running. That sizing decision drives the capital cost. Whether you draw 5kWh on a Tuesday morning in May is irrelevant to network costs. Whether you draw 3kW at 6pm on that February evening is extremely relevant.

What peak demand charging looks like in practice

A demand-based charge bills households for their maximum kilowatt draw in a billing period, or their draw during defined peak windows — not their total kilowatt-hours consumed. This is not a foreign concept: Ausgrid's EA116 residential demand tariff already does exactly this — charging based on the maximum half-hourly demand in the 3pm–9pm window, every day during peak seasons (June–August and November–March). SA Power Networks operates similar demand tariff structures.

The technical pre-condition — interval metering — is already largely met. Over 56% of NEM meters are already remotely read; Victoria is at 99%. The AEMC's accelerated smart meter rollout (mandatory by December 2030) started in December 2025. For smart meters installed from December 2025, customers have a two-year consent-protected window before retailers can move them to a new tariff structure. The infrastructure for demand tariffs is not a 2030 problem — it's a now problem for more than half the market.

Peak demand charges preserve solar's value while funding the network more efficiently.

A household with rooftop solar still draws from the grid at peak evening times — panels aren't generating then. Under peak demand pricing, they pay for that peak draw, contributing fairly to network costs. But their daytime self-consumption doesn't generate charges, preserving the bill savings that made solar worth installing. The investment case for solar remains intact; the cross-subsidy is addressed at the right point in time.

Why the AEMC's dynamic pricing gets there — eventually

The AEMC's long-run vision (dynamic congestion pricing that's zero when the network is unconstrained, priced when constrained) is essentially a real-time version of peak demand pricing. The AEMC's report says this is the "first-best mechanism." The problem is that getting there takes 10+ years, and the intermediate path runs through a period of higher flat access charges. Demand-based tariffs, which networks can already implement under current rules, could compress that transition timeline significantly — without the distributional disruption of a flat charge phase.

The international evidence: what happens when you actually try this

There is no jurisdiction that has implemented exactly this reform — a shift from volumetric to fixed network access charges — with high rooftop solar penetration and has accumulated post-implementation distributional data. But two cases provide the closest available evidence, and Australian modelling fills the gap for the local context.

California (AB 205, 2024–present): the closest structural comparator — but too new to have outcome data

California has the closest profile to Australia: high rooftop solar penetration (~30%), a large low-income subsidy program (CARE/FERA), and a regulator that explicitly designed income graduation into the fixed charge from the start. Under AB 205 (2022), the CPUC approved an income-graduated fixed charge in July 2024:

$6/mo
CARE households (lowest income). Paired with ~5–7¢/kWh volumetric reduction — revenue neutral, net benefit for high-consumption poor households
$12/mo
FERA households (moderate low-income). Same volumetric reduction applies across all tiers
$24.15/mo
All other residential customers (general market). Revenue-neutral offset against lower per-kWh rates — but solar owners who self-consume see reduced grid charges that no longer offset the new fixed cost

SDG&E implemented the charge in October 2025, SCE in November 2025, and PG&E in March 2026. The reform is less than 9 months old at the time of writing. No post-implementation distributional outcome data exists yet. A legislative push to reverse the reform (21 coastal Democrats plus Senate Republicans introduced bills) was "put on ice" by legislative leadership, but the political pressure signals that real-world impacts are already being felt.

What California reveals before outcomes are in: income graduation requires income data

The CPUC acknowledged it could not fully income-graduate charges because California legislation "did not include authority to access income data from the state tax authority." Without tax data, income graduation relies on CARE/FERA program enrollment as a proxy — a proxy that misses the working poor above the CARE eligibility threshold. The AEMC faces exactly the same constraint: it has no authority to access ATO income data. Any income-graduated Australian access charge would start from the same implementation gap.

UK Targeted Charging Review (2019–2022): the only case with actual outcome data

The UK shifted electricity network costs from unit rates to standing charges via the TCR — the same structural mechanism as the AEMC's proposal. UK rooftop solar penetration is around 5–6%, very different from Australia's 33%, so the UK case is not a direct solar equity analogy. But it provides the only jurisdiction with real post-implementation distributional data for this type of reform.

UK outcome: standing charges rose 130%, and Ofgem is now consulting on reversal

UK electricity standing charges rose from 22p/day (October 2019) to 51p/day (July 2025) — 130% in seven years. The average low-income consumer gained only £21.90/year from the volumetric reduction. But 1.2 million low-income consumers with high energy needs — medical equipment, electric heating, or poorly insulated homes (disproportionately private renters) — paid £44.52 more per year.

Ofgem has since opened: an August 2024 options paper on reducing standing charges, a February 2025 consultation on a zero standing charge tariff option, and a September 2025 proposal requiring suppliers to offer at least one lower standing charge alternative. The distributional arithmetic — fixed charges take proportionally more from low-consumption households — does not depend on solar penetration. It holds here too.

Australian modelling: what IEEFA found for the AEMC's specific proposal

In the absence of international post-implementation data for a high-solar context, the most directly relevant quantitative evidence is IEEFA Australia's modelling of the AEMC's draft proposal (February 2026). IEEFA modelled the shift to predominantly fixed network tariffs across NEM capitals and found:

Bill impacts (IEEFA, Feb 2026)

Low- and median-consuming households pay more; high-consuming households pay less.

Homes with rooftop solar: bill increases of $239–$564/year.

Battery owners (10kWh): additional costs of $5,800–$11,500 over battery lifetime — more than cancelling the ~$3,100–$3,300 federal rebate under the Cheaper Home Batteries Program (as of February 2026 when IEEFA published; rebate has since decreased).

The AEMC's own modelling

AEMC scenario modelling (April 2026): low-consumption small business "Nina the Florist" faces up to $810/year higher bills by 2040.

Green Energy Markets: low-income consumers face $127–$217/year increases across networks — Tristan Edis: "Robin Hood scheme in reverse."

Consumer protection design outsourced to HoustonKemp — options identified, not specified. Protections to be designed after the charge structure is locked in.

No jurisdiction with high solar penetration has implemented this reform and survived the distributional scrutiny. The burden of proof is on the AEMC.

California is the closest structural comparator — same reform type, high solar — but only months old and already under political pressure. The UK has outcome data but a different solar market. Both point in the same direction: moving costs into fixed charges creates new losers among low-consumption and low-income households that are hard to protect after the fact. Australian-specific modelling (IEEFA, AEMC's own scenarios) confirms the same distributional arithmetic applies here. The AEMC has not demonstrated why Australia's outcome would differ — and that gap in the case should be addressed before the Final Report.

The AEMC's three problems need three separate interventions — not one blunt instrument.

Renters need community solar and tenancy standards — a property and housing policy response. Apartments need virtual net metering and community solar access. The network funding gap needs charges that track what drives costs — connection capacity or peak demand, not flat access — with differentiation that means smaller, lower-impact connections pay less. Applied together, these three solutions address the AEMC's equity goals without eroding the solar investment that lower-income suburban Australia made over the past 15 years.

The policy ask: model these alternatives before locking in the access charge approach.

The AEMC commissioned HoustonKemp to model consumer protection options for its preferred approach. An equivalent analysis modelling community solar uptake, connection capacity differentiation, and demand-based tariffs would show whether the equity and efficiency goals can be better achieved without a flat charge transition. The distributional data to run this analysis already exists in the CER, APVI, and ABS datasets.

  11. UK Targeted Charging Review — outcome data▶
    Ofgem Targeted Charging Review (TCR), implemented 2019–2022: shifted residual network costs from unit rates to standing charges. Electricity standing charges rose from 22p/day (October 2019) to 51p/day (July 2025) — a 130% increase in seven years. Distributional impact: 5.5 million low-income consumers gained average £21.90/year from volumetric reduction; 1.2 million low-income consumers with high energy needs (medical equipment, electric heating, poorly insulated homes) paid £44.52 more per year. (Ofgem, 2024; Citizens Advice response to Ofgem Call for Input on Standing Charges, 2024.) Ofgem subsequently: opened an August 2024 options paper on reducing standing charges; February 2025 consultation on a zero standing charge price cap tariff option; September 2025 proposal requiring suppliers to offer at least one lower standing charge tariff. Note: UK rooftop solar penetration (~5–6%) is significantly lower than Australia (~33%), limiting direct solar-equity comparability. The UK case is cited as mechanism evidence for the distributional consequences of shifting costs into fixed charges.
  12. Australian distributional modelling — IEEFA, AEMC, Green Energy Markets▶
    IEEFA Australia briefing note (Jay Gordon, February 2026): modelled shift to predominantly fixed network tariffs across NEM capitals. Findings: low- and median-consuming households pay more; high-consuming households pay less; homes with rooftop solar face bill increases of $239–$564/year; 10kWh battery owners face $5,800–$11,500 additional costs over battery lifetime, exceeding the Cheaper Home Batteries Program rebate (~$3,100–$3,300 at February 2026 — rebate structure revised downward from May 2026); payback periods for new 8kW solar + 10kWh battery increase by 1.2–4.4 years. IEEFA calls for a first-principles review of electricity network economic regulation before any tariff changes. (ieefa.org, "Network tariff shake-up could create more problems than it solves.") AEMC scenario modelling (April 2026): "Nina the Florist" scenario (low-consumption small business, Sydney) faces up to $810/year higher bills by 2040 under reform. Green Energy Markets analysis (Tristan Edis, RenewEconomy, April 2026): low-income consumers face $127–$217/year increases across NEM networks. HoustonKemp consumer protection options report (AEMC, April 2026): identifies protection options but does not specify final mechanisms. Rewiring Australia (February 2026): argues property-value-scaled fixed charges levied on owners (not renters), combined with dynamic network pricing, would be preferable to flat fixed charges. All population and dwelling data are 2021 Census base.
